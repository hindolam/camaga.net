Critiquing the Carnatic Music Critic

 

As one of the organizers of Carnatic music concerts in the Atlanta area, I frequently hear about the shortcomings of a particular concert from the music connoisseurs in the audience. The list can include everything from: incorrect swara prastharas to the mridangist inappropriately chewing betel nuts during raga alapana.  But, during the same concert, I also hear from the “simpler” members of the audience on how great the concert was and how the artist’s rendering of a kriti on Lord Muruga or Lord Venkateshwara brought tears to their eyes. One concert. Multiple opinions; perhaps, that is the greatness of our music: each can approach and relate to it in his/her own personal way.  Regardless, it raises a fundamental question: does a greater knowledge of Carnatic music lead to greater appreciation of the music? 

A person acquires knowledge through learning, experience and reasoning. Knowledge helps a rasika in understanding the nuances of the music. The rasika becomes  capable of differentiating a good performance from a poor performance.  He recognizes the swara kalpana that is like a garland of beautiful flowers and understands the mathematical computations that exhibit dexterity and control over thala. When a knowledgeable rasika also recognizes that listening to Carnatic music is akin to a pursuit of bhakthi and devotion, the rasika begins to immerse himself mentally and spiritually with the music and does not allow himself to be distracted by a few grammatical or structural transgressions by the performer.  With greater knowledge, the rasika no longer dwells on the mistakes but instead focuses on the endogenous sounds, or the mooladharaja naadha that leads the rasika to harmony and peace. But, these days, such a rasika is getting to be an extinct species.  Today, knowledge means the ability to find a minimum of ten mistakes in the most perfectly rendered concert.  And, if a naive member of the audience had the misfortune to express that an artist performed well, the “scholar-rasika” must put the “naïve-rasika” in his place and point out the lapses in sruthi and the improper prayagoams that the artist made.  Otherwise, the scholar-rasika would be failing in his duties as the gate keeper of quality Carnatic music.

The number of self-appointed scholar-rasikas has grown significantly with the popularity of the Internet.  To certain extent, this is also because Carnatic music is complex and lends itself to analysis and like all music, to perceptions and subjective interpretations.  And, if a critic is to establish his/her worth, he must prove his own expertise and what better way to do this than through an exhibition of knowledge about everything from theory, to the artists, to the sabhas. Consequently, With so many music discussion forums on the Internet, eevery rasika with access to a computer begins has become a music scholar and is free to comment on express his/her opinion on everthing from the appropriateness of kaisiki nishadam in a raga to the staleness of the chutney at the sabha canteen. I am not bemoaning critics who express their opinions or point out why a conccert was less than satisfactory. But, the critic should do so without being hurtful or vituperative. For example, I read a critique recently where the critic not only commented on the performance but also about the performer; starting with a comment on why the color of the artist’s jibba was too flashy, the critic then provides a list of mistakes made by the artist and then advises that youngsters of this world to never take a music lesson from this particular artist. In a three-hour concert, the critic found nothing worth listening to. Like most critics, this one was not brave enough to sign his whole name but rather provided a pseudonym below his comments. Would the same critic be so blunt if he/she were to sign his or her true name instead of names such as “Carnatic Einstein,”  “Sangeetha Goonda,” or “Music Mafia.” 

My intention is to neither defend the professional musicians from criticism nor to argue that professional musicians rarely make mistakes.  Indeed, if professional musicians do a half-hearted job, critics have every right to point out why the audience was not satisfied with the performance. A scholarly critique is useful to an artist to learn how to improve his/her own performance. But, such criticism should be balanced: it should include both positive feeback and suggestions for  improving future performances and it must avoid personal comments.  The critics must also recognize that no concert performance, however reputed the artist is, can be perfect and no concert, however inexperienced an artist is, can be terrible.  Every concert will have its pleasing moments and therefore, as critics, we should be impartial and charitable.  How many of us can claim that at least one day during the years we have worked in our chosen profession, we have performed perfectly every time we went to work?  I am willing to accept that I have never been that perfect. And yet, when it comes to our musicians, we hold them to higher standards of performance than we subject ourselves to.

Let me move away from my own tirade of criticism and negativity to something more valuable.  There is a famous saying that Carnatic music should be listened to and enjoyed and not to be read or analyzed.  This is because Carnatic music is the pleasing art of sound and it is also a spiritual endeavour or “nadopasana.” Both the performer and the listener must involve themselves in such nadopasana. As Saint Thyagaraja says in the Sankarabharanam kriti, Swara Raga Sudharasa, “Mooladaraja NAada Merungute Mudamagu Mokshamura”.  By listening to the seven swaras and identifying ourselves with the swaras, we are in consonance with our inner spirits and there is no better way to salvation or moksha than through such harmony. As a rasika, if we let our mind be occupied by the swaras (instead of the color of the artist’s jibba) and if we travel in the chariot of swaras or “Sapthaswara Chaari” (See: Sri Thyagaraja’s Sribapriya Sangeethopasana in Atana), we will realize the spiritual experience that comes with listening to our music – even if the performance was less than optimal. 

In his kritis, Saint Thyagaraja sends a message to both the performers and the critics.  To the performers, he says that music should be rendered with humility, vinaya and most importantly, devotion. In the Sankarabharanam kriti, “Bhakthi Bhiksha Meeyavayya,” Thyagaraja Swami gives the analogy that the most well rendered song, if sung without devotion is like brocades and diamonds on a dead man - it will neither please the performer nor the audience.  As for the critics, Swami says that the benefit of Sangeetha Sastra Gnana is not merely the ability to recognize mistakes.  Thyagaraja Swami chides us and says that we should not acquire knowledge that does not improve our inner qualities. A very well read man who has not benefited from his erudition and knowledge is like a donkey that carries a load of books. The donkey does not understand the true messages contained in those books.  Like the performers, as critics, we also need humility and vinaya and we must place bhakthi above critical analysis.  As Thyagaraja Swami points in the Kalyani kriti “Bhajana Seyave”, why debate and discuss endlessly when the true purpose of music is immersing ourselves in Nama Sankeerthana.  We acquire scholarliness only when we receive benefits such as: “Prema, Bhakthi, Sujana, Vathsalyamu, Nema Nishta Yesodhanamu” or love, devotion, blessings of elders, grace of God, pure thought, and valuable recognition as a good human being (Sangeetha Sastra Gnanamu – Mukhari).  Let us stay away from negativity the Internet and strive towards harmony.[S1]
