Discarding religion – becoming spiritual

In the US, church attendance is on the decline. To a survey response, pastors of US churches stated that attendance in their churches has declined by over 60 per cent. Is the declining attendance a signal that more Americans are losing faith in their religion? Not necessarily. However, more Americans now assert that they do not need membership in a religion or attend church services to show that they are living a life of faith. These religious skeptics known as, ‘Spiritual, But Not Religious’ or ‘SBNR’, are unhappy with the assertions of their religious leaders that only membership in their religion leads to salvation and believing in other religions leads to damnation. The SBNRs also question religious leaders who assert that God will forgive all of their sins only if they are affiliated with their religion. Frustrated by claims such as these, one of the SBNRs expressed, “I don’t need to define myself to any community by putting myself in a box labeled Baptist, or Catholic, or Muslim. When I die, I believe all my accounting will be done to God, and that when I enter the eternal realm, I will not walk through a door with a label on it.”

The SBNRs distrust religious leaders who make exaggerated claims to establish the supremacy of their religions and to promote blind faith, dogmas, and ritualistic conformity. They also refuse to believe that membership in a specific religion guarantees spiritual realization. The SBNRs assert that spiritual realization is not a membership benefit but an intuitive experience that transcends institutions, creeds, and rituals. A religion can only help the spiritual endeavor by encouraging one to look inside, by asking one to free the mind from orthodox or authoritarian beliefs, and by encouraging one to seek the God within. More importantly, the SBNRs argue, no religion should act as a gatekeeper and declare that it has the power to decide who can enter the spiritual domain and who cannot.

What is interesting about the SBNRs is, just like the members of other religious faiths, the SBNRs are also searching, as humans, for meaning, truth, and an understanding of who we are. These questions have perplexed humanity for thousands of years. The attempts to find answers to these questions have traditionally evolved into religions.

Religions originated when humans developed belief in the existence of a divine power (God). The earliest recorded belief in a divine power is traceable to Hinduism (about 2,500-3,000 B.C.). Later, similar beliefs in a divine power arose also among the peoples of Babylonia, Egypt, Greece, and Rome. While the faith in a divine power grew independently among the various cultures, each culture nevertheless believed that God is everything and everything is God. But, they differed in the way they described God and what constituted God’s divine declarations.

For example, in Christianity, there is only one God who manifests as the Father, Son and the Holy Spirit. The Bible is the only inspired, infallible, and authoritative Word of God. Islam similarly believes that there is no God but Allah. A true Muslim must believe in the six articles of faith and accept only Islam as the true religion. In contrast, ancient religions such as Hinduism or Buddhism do not claim divine revelations or demand that its followers adhere to a chosen God. In Hindu religions, an individual is free to worship God in many forms. According to Hindu religious texts, ultimately, the various forms of God are nothing but different conceptions of one formless supreme reality, and religion is more a way of life than a faith to be strictly believed or a divine statement to be accepted without questioning. Hindu religions do not claim any scripture or book as the sole authoritative statement from God. Even the revered Gita and the Vedas do not claim that they are infallible or all-inclusive. On the contrary, the Gita and the Vedas specifically emphasize that spiritual experience is far superior to the words written about it.

Regardless of how they describe God or what represents scriptural authority, all religions speak of a universal code of ethics. Every religion asks its followers to not lie, steal, or kill, but to be compassionate, kind and foster humanity. These core values form the basis for introspection and intuition and guides one towards the path of spiritual emancipation. Even the most skeptical SBNR probably espouses these same values because they are the framework for human existence. However, the point of disagreement for SBNRs occurs when they reject authoritarian religious dogma. The requirements, for example, that a true Christian attend church services or a devout Muslim worship five times a day, are dogmas and are not universal Truths. Rituals and rules for religious practices, dogmas, represent the opinions of humans, not God. Dogmas are useful to the extent that they bring people together under a common tent, and give them support and direction in dealing with human dilemmas. However, when dogmas become the primary focus of a religion or when they become tools that divide communities or become excuses for claiming superiority over others, the dogmas give rise to bias, prejudices, and hatred and undermine the very religion from which they originated. “If we surround our souls with a shell, a national pride, racial superiority, frozen articles of faith and empty presumption of castes and classes, we stifle and suppress the breath of the spirit.”

While it may be true that rituals and worship modes prevent an individual from accessing God on his/her own terms, they are also required to raise an individual above his immediate reality, and to take him closer to spiritual reality. As the Vedas state, “God is not the ideal we cherish or worship, but the real we apprehend. Spiritual experience is not a species of imaginative thinking (or myth and miracles), but is the closest communion with Reality.”

But, spiritual experience does not arise in a vacuum. Religion is the process that leads to spiritual experience. Religion, through imagery, mythology, rituals, and stories, creates a path and lets one travel towards the spiritual destination. As Dr S. Radhakrishnan says, “We require (religious) imagery to clothe the (spiritual) vision.” Therefore, one must sustain religious faith, and the forms and conceptions that religion provides, to interpret reality. When one attains spiritual emancipation, the most comprehensive of religious forms and conceptions become inadequate to describe that experience. Until one reaches the spiritual destination, one cannot afford to ignore the religious path.

Across the planet, humans contend with war, conflict, crushing poverty and unspeakable injustice. And more often than not, they turn to religion to guide them through a “…change of consciousness, a rebornness, and an inner evolution, and to deal with the issues that confront humanity.”

Whether one believes in God, spirituality, or nothing, something in our nature as humans asks us to look beyond ourselves towards a greater power. And whether this power is found in religion, spirituality or from within, it unites us in one common struggle – the struggle to understand, love and live peacefully. Regardless of how we get there, the ultimate goal is to be better than we are. It is time to de-emphasize the labels and emphasize the common purposes.
